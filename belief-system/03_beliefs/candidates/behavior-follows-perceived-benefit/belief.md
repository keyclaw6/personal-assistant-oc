# Belief: Behavior follows perceived benefit

## Current Belief

I tend to keep doing what appears to give me the most benefit, least pain, or best expected outcome from inside my current understanding.

## Desired Direction

Use this as a practical lens for understanding behavior without shame or force.

## Where It Shows Up

- Habits.
- Avoidance.
- Social behavior.
- Motivation.
- Reading and learning.
- Choosing between short-term and long-term options.

## Preference Logic

If this belief is true, then the useful question is not only "How do I force myself to stop?" but "What makes this option appear best right now?"

## Protective Function

This belief protects agency and makes behavior understandable.

## Cost

If overused, it could become too rationalistic or ignore physical, emotional, environmental, or medical constraints.

## Evidence For

- User stated this as a core belief.
- The Freedom Model source folder uses a similar preference-change lens.

## Evidence Against

- Some behavior may be strongly shaped by physiology, environment, trauma, social pressure, or impaired state.
- "Perceived best" may be nonverbal, emotional, or automatic rather than consciously reasoned.

## Replacement Belief Candidates

- My behavior often follows perceived benefit, but perceived benefit can be distorted, embodied, automatic, or context-bound.
- To change behavior, I can update understanding, change environment, change identity, or reduce the old option's perceived benefit.

## Daily-Life Implications

If I actually understood this belief, then in normal life I would:

- Inspect perceived benefits before trying to force change.
- Ask what an unwanted behavior is doing for me.
- Design experiments that change preference, not only discipline.
- Avoid shame as a primary change tool.
